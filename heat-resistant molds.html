Exploring Heat-Resistant Molds in Processed Fruit Products: The Role of Processing Conditions in Ascospore Growth.

Research Assistantship

Abstract: Heat-resistant molds (HRM) possess significant threat to food industries due to their ability to survive high temperatures and develop in a variety of environments. To understand how inhibiting variables affect HRM growth, this study evaluated the impact of high sugar concentration, low pH, and temperature on the development of three HRM strains isolated from fruit products. The study found that all HRM strains had relatively short times to develop detectable colonies at 50 °Brix, while no visible growth was observed at 60 °Brix. B. nivea was found to be the most susceptible to low temperatures, requiring up to 43 days to develop detectable colonies at 10 °C and unable to grow at colder temperatures. The tv varied from 6 to 27 days based on pH and isolates. HRM's resistance to high sugar concentrations and ability to grow in cold environments pose a threat to the microbiological safety of fruit products. The evaluated data were fitted to several statistical distributions to support further research on the quantitative microbiological risk in pasteurized fruit products. This study provides valuable insights into how inhibiting variables affect HRM development and can inform efforts to minimize their impact on fruit-based product quality and safety.
